### SECTION A: PRIMARY SOURCES CORPUS

#### A1. Literary Sources

**Kojiki** (古事記, "Record of Ancient Matters") — 712 CE
- Compiled by Ō no Yasumaro from the oral recitations of Hieda no Are, by order of Empress Genmei
- Contains the mythological wrestling match between the kami Takemikazuchi and Takeminakata, considered the divine origin of sumo
- Takeminakata bore a heavy boulder on his fingertips, challenging Takemikazuchi to a contest of strength. When he tried to seize Takemikazuchi's arm, it transformed into an icicle, then a sword blade. Takemikazuchi then grasped Takeminakata's arm, "crushed it like a reed and threw it aside." Takeminakata fled to Suwa in Shinano, where he surrendered.
- This is the *kuni-yuzuri* (transfer of the land) myth — possession of the Japanese islands decided by wrestling
- Significance: establishes wrestling as the method by which cosmic sovereignty is decided. Not sport — divine right through physical contest.
- Public domain translation: Basil Hall Chamberlain (1882), available via sacred-texts.com and Project Gutenberg

**Nihon Shoki** (日本書紀, "Chronicles of Japan") — 720 CE
- Official court history compiled by Prince Toneri and Ō no Yasumaro
- Book VI (Emperor Suinin, dated 23 BCE in the chronicle's own chronology): First recorded match between mortals — **Nomi no Sukune vs. Taima no Kehaya (Kuyehaya)**
- From W.G. Aston translation (1896): courtiers informed the Emperor of Kuyehaya of Taima, who boasted of his unmatched strength. A minister suggested summoning Nomi no Sukune from Izumo. The two faced each other and kicked simultaneously. *"Nomi no Sukune broke with a kick the ribs of Kuyehaya and also kicked and broke his loins and thus killed him."*
- Aftermath: "the land of Taima no Kuyehaya was seized, and was all given to Nomi no Sukune." A location called Koshi-ore-da ("field of broken loins") commemorated the event.
- Nomi no Sukune became the legendary ancestor of sumo wrestlers and was later deified as the patron of potters (he also suggested replacing human sacrifice with clay figures — *haniwa*)
- Public domain translation: W.G. Aston, *Nihongi: Chronicles of Japan from the Earliest Times to A.D. 697* (1896), available on Internet Archive and Wikisource
- Key caveat: the 23 BCE dating is the chronicle's internal chronology, not independently verified. The text was compiled in 720 CE, ~740 years after the events described. Like Pausanias writing about Arrhichion.

**Sumai no Sechie records** — Nara & Heian periods (8th–12th century)
- Sechie-zumo (節会相撲) was the formal sumo tournament held at the imperial court
- First recorded sechie-zumo: 726 CE under Emperor Shōmu (Nara period)
- Institutionalized during the Heian period (794–1185) as an annual court ceremony
- Format: wrestlers selected from provinces, competed as members of left or right imperial guard
- Function was simultaneously religious (divination for harvest), political (provincial prestige), and entertainment
- Modern sumo preserves ritual elements: salt-tossing (ring purification), chikara-mizu (power water), the dohyō design itself
- Sources: various court records and later compilations; no single canonical text

**Takenouchi Keisho Kogo Den** — founding document of Takenouchi-ryū (c. 1532+)
- Records the establishment and development of the school by Takenouchi Chūnagon Daijō Nakatsukasadaiyū Hisamori
- Founded 1532 (first year of Tenbun) at Sannomiya shrine in Mimasaka Province (modern Okayama)
- Founding legend: Hisamori encountered a yamabushi (mountain ascetic) who defeated him and taught him that "when you meet the enemy, in that instant, life and death are decided." The priest broke Hisamori's bokken into two daggers, teaching him *kogusoku* (armed grappling) and rope-binding
- Curriculum: jūjutsu, bōjutsu, kenjutsu, iaijutsu, naginatajutsu, tessenjutsu, hojōjutsu (rope binding), sakkatsuhō (resuscitation)
- Widely cited as the oldest documented jujutsu school
- Together with Yōshin-ryū and Ryōi Shintō-ryū, one of the three most influential jūjutsu schools of the Edo period
- Transmission through *densho* (伝書, secret transmission scrolls) — initially limited to family members
- 14 headmasters documented; current (14th): Takenouchi Tōichirō Hisamune
- Key branches: Bitchū Den (Okayama), Donteki-ryū, Takeuchi Santo-ryū
- Influenced numerous derivative schools including Fusen-ryū, Sōsuishi-ryū, Takagi-ryū, Hontai Yōshin-ryū

**Kano Jigoro's writings** — late 19th–early 20th century
- *Kodokan Judo* (edited under Kodokan Editorial Committee, Kodansha International, 1986) — the definitive technical and philosophical treatise
- *Mind Over Muscle: Writings from the Founder of Judo* (Kodansha USA, 2005) — English anthology
- *Kanō Jigorō chosakushū* (五月書房, 1983) — collected Japanese works
- "Jiujitsu: The Old Samurai Art of Fighting without Weapons" — lecture to the Asiatic Society of Japan, 18 April 1888 (co-authored with Thomas Lindsay)
- "The Contribution of Judo to Education" — lecture at University of Southern California, 1932
- "Principles of Judo and Their Applications to All Phases of Human Activity" — Athens lecture, 1934
- Kano's core philosophy, stated in his own words:
  - 精力善用 (*seiryoku zen'yō*) — "maximum efficiency, minimum effort"
  - 自他共栄 (*jita kyōei*) — "mutual welfare and benefit"
  - "Judo is the way of the highest or most efficient use of both physical and mental energy" (1915)
  - "When yielding is the highest efficient use of energy, then yielding is judo" (1938, shortly before his death)
- Three aspects of judo training: *rentai-hō* (physical exercise), *shōbu-hō* (martial art/competition), *shushin-hō* (cultivation of wisdom and virtue)

**Daito-ryu transmission scrolls** — 1899 onwards
- *Hiden mokuroku* (秘伝目録): 88 explicitly written items + 30 "not written" = 118 total techniques. Created 1899 when Takeda Sokaku began teaching.
- *Hiden okugi no koto* (秘伝奧儀之事): 36 items, highest transmission level initially
- *Aiki no jutsu 30 kajo*: First appeared in Ueshiba Morihei's August 1922 certificate. The 30 "unwritten" items from the hiden mokuroku, now explicitly named as "aiki" techniques.
- *Soden* (Daito-ryu Aiki-budo Densho Zen Juikkan): compilation held by the Takumakai, the most comprehensive technical document
- Takeda Sokaku's *eimeiroku* (enrollment records): detailed student logs; critical primary source for reconstructing teaching history. The 1922 entry for Ueshiba marks the first written appearance of "Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu" — previously always "Daito-ryu Jujutsu."
- Guillaume Erard's research (guillaumeerard.com) provides the most detailed scholarly analysis of these documents in English

#### A2. Key Historical Events & Figures

**Mythological period (pre-history)**
- Takemikazuchi vs. Takeminakata — divine wrestling for sovereignty (Kojiki, 712 CE)
- Nomi no Sukune vs. Taima no Kehaya — first mortal match, 23 BCE per Nihon Shoki dating (recorded 720 CE)

**Nara/Heian period (710–1185)**
- 726 CE: First recorded sechie-zumo under Emperor Shōmu
- Sumo institutionalized as court ceremony; ritual, divinatory, and political functions
- Wrestlers drawn from provinces; competed as left/right imperial guard

**Sengoku period (1467–1615)**
- 1532: Takenouchi-ryū founded — first documented jujutsu school
- 1588: Toyotomi Hideyoshi's *katanagari* (sword hunt) — confiscation of weapons from non-samurai classes. Chief goal: prevent rōnin uprisings and distinguish ruling samurai class. One district surrendered ~1,100 long swords, 1,500 short swords, 500 guns, 700 knives.
- Connection to jujutsu development: disarmament of the general population created conditions where unarmed combat systems became relevant beyond battlefield supplement. The Takenouchi-ryū predates the sword hunt by 56 years, but the sword hunt accelerated the broader proliferation of civilian jujutsu schools.

**Edo period (1603–1868)**
- Over 700 documented koryu jujutsu schools during this period
- Chin Genpin (Chen Yuanbin, 1587–1671): Chinese martial artist who taught Chinese kenpo in Japan from 1619. His influence on jujutsu development is debated — some schools claim connection, but the extent of Chinese transmission is unclear.
- Key schools: Takenouchi-ryū, Yōshin-ryū, Ryōi Shintō-ryū, Sekiguchi-ryū, Kito-ryū, Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū
- Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū (天神真楊流): Founded 1830s by Iso Mataemon Ryūkansai Minamoto no Masatari. Amalgamation of Yōshin-ryū and Shin no Shintō-ryū. Known for *atemi waza* (strikes to vital points) and *ne waza* (ground techniques). One of the two largest koryu schools of the late Edo period.
- Kitō-ryū (起倒流): Founded mid-17th century by Fukuno Shichirōemon. Renowned for nage-waza (throwing) and the principles of *ki* (rising) and *tō* (falling). The kuzushi principle (balance breaking before throwing) originated here.

**Meiji period (1868–1912)**
- **Kano Jigoro** (1860–1938): The central figure
  - 1877: Began studying Tenjin Shin'yō-ryū under Fukuda Hachinosuke
  - 1879: Fukuda died; Kano inherited school scrolls. Continued under Iso Masatomo (3rd-generation headmaster). Iso died 1881.
  - 1881: Began studying Kitō-ryū under Iikubo Tsunetoshi (Kōnen)
  - May/June 1882: Founded Kodokan at Eishō-ji temple, Shitaya ward, Tokyo. Twelve mats. Fewer than a dozen students.
  - October 1883: Received *menkyo* (teaching license) in Kitō-ryū. Later granted *menkyo kaiden* (full transmission).
  - The kuzushi breakthrough: Kano told Iikubo that the throw should be applied after breaking the opponent's posture. Iikubo replied: *"This is right. I am afraid I have nothing more to teach you."*
  - 5 August 1879: Demonstrated jūjutsu for former U.S. President Ulysses S. Grant
  - 18 April 1888: Lectured on "Jiujitsu: The Old Samurai Art of Fighting without Weapons" to Asiatic Society of Japan

- **1886 Tokyo Metropolitan Police Tournament**: The decisive event
  - Organized by Michitsune Mishima, prefect of the Tokyo Metropolitan Police (1885–1888)
  - Kodokan vs. Totsuka-ha Yōshin-ryū (the largest remaining classical school)
  - Result: Kodokan won 13 of 15 bouts with 2 draws (some sources say 15 victories, 0 defeats)
  - Yokoyama Sakujiro's 55-minute bout with Nakamura Hansuke: longest judo match on record
  - Yoshiaki Yamashita defeated Taro Terushima with seoi-nage
  - Impact: transformed judo from an obscure school to Japan's dominant martial art. The police adopted Kodokan judo as their official hand-to-hand system.

- **Fusen-ryū challenge matches** (~1891–1905): The ne waza shock
  - Mataemon Tanabe (1869–1942): master of Fusen-ryū
  - January 1891: Tanabe defeated 3rd-dan Kodokan judoka Takisaburo Tobari. Reversed an osoto makikomi, pinned with kami-shiho-gatame, then choked unconscious with juji-jime.
  - Spring 1892: Rematch. Tobari tried to engage on the ground. Tanabe patiently exhausted him, then choked him again with juji-jime.
  - Strategic insight: identify the phase where the opponent is weakest, take the fight there. The Kodokan had almost no ground grappling before 1900.
  - Consequence: Kano invited Tanabe to teach ne waza to the Kodokan. A ne waza explosion followed (1900–1925). This is the origin of the "ne-waza of the Kansai region."
  - Ironic outcome: Fusen-ryū faded into obscurity — absorbed by the institution it had defeated.

- **Takeda Sokaku** (1859–1943) and Daito-ryu
  - Began martial training under Shibuya Toma in Onoha Itto-ryu kenjutsu at age 10
  - 1876: Received menkyo at age 17
  - 1898: Pivotal meeting with Hoshina Chikanori, who advised him to abandon swordsmanship and teach jujutsu
  - 1899: Began teaching Daito-ryu Jujutsu at age 40. ~30,000 students during his lifetime.
  - The concept of *aiki*: originally negative in Edo-period usage (collision of attacking impulses). Sokaku reinterpreted it positively. The 1892 publication "Budo Hiketsu: Aiki no Jutsu" defined Aiki as "the highest level mystery of Japanese Budo."
  - Sagawa Nenokichi's notes (c. 1913): first documented evidence of Aiki as technical term in Sokaku's teaching, written phonetically (アイキ) when encountering unfamiliar vocabulary.

- **Maeda Mitsuyo** (1878–1941): "Conde Koma" (Count Combat)
  - First Kodokan record: 1897. Achieved 2nd dan within two years.
  - Student of Kano; sent abroad to promote judo
  - Toured Europe: England, Belgium, Spain. Competed in catch wrestling matches without gi.
  - Absorbed catch wrestling techniques: half-nelson, hammer-lock, no-gi control positions, leg attacks
  - Arrived in Brazil September 1914 (demonstration at Teatro Variedades, Santos, per *Correio Paulistano*)
  - 1921: Founded first judo academy in Brazil
  - Said to have engaged in 1,000+ challenge matches, never losing in judo-style competition
  - 27 November 1941: Kodokan promoted him to 7th dan. He died the next day.
  - Legacy: Carlos Gracie received from Maeda a system already synthesizing Kodokan judo, Fusen-ryū ne waza, and catch wrestling. BJJ's hybrid character was baked in from the source.

**Taishō/Early Shōwa period (1912–1945)**
- **Ueshiba Morihei** (1883–1969) and the birth of aikido
  - March 1915: Met Takeda Sokaku at the Hisada Inn, Engaru. Enrolled in Daito-ryu.
  - 1916: Received hiden mokuroku and hiden okugi no koto scrolls
  - Fall 1920: Established Ueshiba Juku dojo in Ayabe, Kyoto (with Ōmoto-kyō religious community)
  - April–September 1922: Sokaku taught 11 seminars at the dojo
  - August 1922: Received Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu Hiden Okugi certificate — first person to receive certificate including Aiki no Jutsu 30 kajo
  - September 1922: Appointed kyoju dairi (teaching representative) — first in newly named Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu
  - 1920s–mid-1930s: Taught Daito-ryu; students' documents bear the Daito-ryu name
  - Late 1920s onward: began diverging from Daito-ryu, influenced by Ōmoto-kyō spirituality
  - 1937: Official association with Daito-ryu ended
  - 1942: The name "aikido" officially adopted under the Dai Nippon Butoku Kai reorganization
  - Key distinction: Daito-ryu focuses on combat-effective joint locks and throws; aikido increasingly emphasized harmonious movement and spiritual development. This is the "relegation" — a martial system with genuine grappling roots that moved away from adversarial stress-testing.

**Post-war period**
- **1964 Tokyo Olympics**: Judo's Olympic debut
  - Approved at 1960 IOC Session in Rome
  - Competition held at Nippon Budokan (purpose-built)
  - 4 weight classes, men only
  - Japan won first three classes; the "open" weight class was won by Dutchman Anton Geesink — a shock
  - Judo became a permanent Olympic fixture from 1972 onward
  - Significance: the endpoint of the trajectory from Kano's 12-mat temple dojo to global sport

#### A3. Material Evidence

**Woodblock prints (ukiyo-e) — Edo period**
- Numerous sumo-themed ukiyo-e by major artists: Kunisada, Kuniyoshi, Sharaku, Hokusai
- Sumo prints are among the most common subjects in ukiyo-e — wrestlers were celebrities
- Typically CC0/public domain; widely available on Met Museum Open Access, Library of Congress, Wikimedia Commons
- Key search terms: "sumo ukiyo-e", "相撲 浮世絵", "sumo wrestler woodblock print"
- Technical value: show stances, ring rituals, referee positions, audience context. Limited biomechanical detail compared to Greek pottery.

**Koryu densho scrolls — Edo period**
- Illustrated transmission scrolls from jujutsu schools showing techniques
- Most are held by school families or museums in Japan; digital access varies
- The Takenouchi-ryū densho and related school documents are the most important
- Some examples available through academic databases and Japanese digital archives

**Early judo photographs — Meiji/Taishō period**
- Photographs of Kano and early Kodokan students exist from the 1880s onward
- The famous 1906 photograph: Kano with leading koryu masters including Mataemon Tanabe — shows him actively cataloging the classical tradition
- Maeda Mitsuyo: photograph showing him applying a half-nelson and hammer-lock — standard catch wrestling positions, documenting cross-style absorption
- Key collections: Kodokan archives, Japanese sports museums
- CC0 status: most early photographs (pre-1900) are public domain by age; Meiji-era photos generally CC0 in Japan

**Sumo prints from major museums**
- Metropolitan Museum of Art: extensive collection of sumo ukiyo-e under CC0 Open Access
- British Museum: sumo prints available for non-commercial use
- Library of Congress: Japanese prints collection includes sumo subjects
- Search: Met API `collectionapi.metmuseum.org/public/collection/v1/search?q=sumo+wrestling&hasImages=true`

#### A4. Documentary / Institutional Sources

**Kodokan enrollment records** — 1882 onwards
- The Kodokan maintained meticulous records from its founding
- By 1911: over 1,000 dan-graded members
- The growth from 12 mats to 1,200+ mats (1958 eight-story facility) is itself a primary source for institutional history

**Takeda Sokaku's eimeiroku** (enrollment records)
- Detailed student logs maintained by Takeda throughout his teaching career
- Critical for dating the evolution from "Daito-ryu Jujutsu" to "Daito-ryu Aikijujutsu"
- The 1922 entry for Ueshiba is the documentary hinge point

**Police tournament records** — 1886
- Records from the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department
- Primary documentation from neutral observers or the Totsuka school remains scarce or inaccessible
- Kodokan's own records describe 15 bouts; numerical discrepancies exist between sources

**Correio Paulistano newspaper** — 1914
- Records Maeda's judo demonstration at Teatro Variedades, Santos, September 24, 1914
- Primary Brazilian source for dating Maeda's arrival

### SECTION C: CENTERPIECE — THE KANO SYNTHESIS

#### Concept

The centerpiece compares multiple source accounts of how Kano Jigoro synthesized disparate jujutsu traditions into Kodokan judo — the most significant structural transformation in Japanese martial arts history.

#### Matrix Structure: Accounts × Concepts

**Accounts (columns):**
1. Tenshin Shin'yō-ryū legacy (via Fukuda and Iso)
2. Kitō-ryū legacy (via Iikubo Tsunetoshi)
3. Fusen-ryū challenge (via Mataemon Tanabe)
4. Kano's own writings (primary voice)
5. Maeda's extension (export and cross-pollination)

**Concepts (rows):**
1. **Technical contribution**: What each lineage contributed to the Kodokan system
2. **Training methodology**: Kata vs. randori — the methodological revolution
3. **Kuzushi principle**: The balance-breaking insight and who provided it
4. **Ne waza integration**: Ground fighting before and after Fusen-ryū
5. **Philosophical framework**: Seiryoku zen'yō, jita kyōei — where these ideas came from
6. **Institutional response**: How Kano absorbed challenges rather than fighting them

#### Key Comparisons

**Tenshin Shin'yō-ryū → Kodokan:**
- Contributed: atemi waza, ne waza elements, choking techniques
- Kano studied under two masters who both died (Fukuda 1879, Iso 1881) — forcing him to seek new schools
- Preserved in: Kime-no-kata, Katame-no-kata
- Paradox: the ne waza content was richer than most schools of the era, yet Kano initially excluded ground fighting from randori

**Kitō-ryū → Kodokan:**
- Contributed: nage waza (throwing), the kuzushi principle
- The breakthrough moment: Kano explained to Iikubo that throws should follow balance-breaking. Iikubo: "I have nothing more to teach you."
- The word "judo": Kitō-ryū was internally called "Kitō-ryū judo." Kano reframed *dō* (the way) as ongoing self-improvement.
- Preserved in: several Kitō-ryū kata adopted directly into Kodokan curriculum

**Fusen-ryū → Kodokan:**
- The only defeat in Kodokan challenge-match history came via Fusen-ryū submissions on the ground
- Strategic logic: identify the opponent's weakest phase, force the fight there
- Kano's response was institutional genius: invited the victor to teach, not to rematch. Absorbed the innovation.
- This same pattern repeats: Tanabe's strategy → Maeda's fighting circuit → Gracie vale tudo

**Kano's own framework:**
- Three aspects: rentai-hō, shōbu-hō, shushin-hō (physical, martial, moral)
- "Maximum efficiency, minimum effort" as the unifying principle
- The decision to exclude dangerous techniques from randori but preserve them in kata — the safety solution that enabled daily high-intensity training

**Maeda's extension:**
- Carried a system already synthesizing Kodokan throwing, Fusen-ryū ground work, and catch wrestling
- Added no-gi control, different takedown entries, leg attack awareness
- The hybrid character of what became BJJ was present at transmission, not a later development
